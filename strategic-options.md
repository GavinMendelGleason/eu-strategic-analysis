# Strategic Options

Major scenarios for Europe's geopolitical and trade alignment. These are not predictions; they are plausible, structurally distinct futures used to assess supply-chain exposure, industrial-policy posture, and opportunity costs.

## Scenarios

### 1. Transatlantic Renewal

The US and Europe deepen integration, forming a unified trade and defence bloc against Russia and China. Coordinated industrial policy, technology sharing, and mutual market access.

- **Supply chain posture**: Deep transatlantic integration; reduced dependency on both Russia and China.
- **Key risk**: Locked into US foreign-policy priorities; loss of strategic autonomy.

### 2. Europe Pivots to China

Europe decouples from the US and deepens trade and investment ties with China as an alternative pole. Active re-coupling rather than passive decoupling.

- **Supply chain posture**: Chinese manufactured inputs and investment; vulnerable to Beijing's political demands.
- **Key risk**: Replaces one dependency with another; Chinese market access may shrink as China's domestic development progresses.

### 3. Europe Pivots to Russia

Europe decouples from the US and re-couples with Russia for raw materials (energy, minerals, fertilisers) and as a manufacturing destination. Russian raw-material dependency is harder to replace than Chinese market access.

- **Supply chain posture**: Russian raw materials anchor industrial base; reduced Asian manufacturing dependency.
- **Key risk**: Extreme political and reputational cost; sanctions reversal; Russia as an unreliable long-term partner.

### 4. Fragmented Europe

The EU fails to act as a bloc. Eastern members align with the US, some pivot to China, others seek neutrality. Bargaining power collapses through disunity.

- **Supply chain posture**: Patchwork dependencies; no single EU-wide industrial policy. Individual states make separate deals.
- **Key risk**: Race to the bottom; collective weakness in all negotiations.

### 5. Multipolar De-risking

No full decoupling from anyone. Europe maintains diversified, hedged relationships across all major powers. Selective industrial policy in critical sectors. Neither dependency nor decoupling — resilience through redundancy.

- **Supply chain posture**: Multiple supply sources for critical inputs; active management of dependency ratios.
- **Key risk**: Higher input costs; no one partner is fully reliable; complexity of managing competing standards and regulations.

### 6. Russia-US Accommodation at Europe's Expense

The US and Russia reach a grand bargain (e.g. settling Ukraine). The US pivots fully to Asia, leaving Europe to manage Russia on its own. Reduced US trade access compounds the shock.

- **Supply chain posture**: US withdrawal of security umbrella forces rapid European defence industrialisation; ambiguous Russian access to European energy markets.
- **Key risk**: Europe caught between a hostile Russia and an indifferent US with no transition period.

### 7. Forced Autarky

Europe is simultaneously cut off from Russia (military conflict / sanctions escalation) and faces reduced US trade access due to protectionism, while China relations remain cold. Europe is forced into near-complete strategic autonomy.

- **Supply chain posture**: Domestic capacity must replace most external inputs; severe short-term disruption followed by forced industrial transformation.
- **Key risk**: Prolonged economic contraction during the transition; political instability from austerity.

## Cross-Cutting Variables

- **US-Europe relationship** (cooperative ↔ hostile ↔ indifferent)
- **Russia access** (full ↔ partial ↔ severed)
- **China access** (full ↔ conditional ↔ severed)
- **EU cohesion** (unified bloc ↔ fragmented)
- **Time horizon** (sudden shock vs. managed transition)
